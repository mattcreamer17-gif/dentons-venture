How we would think about the VTG stack

Most organizations automate the wrong things. We use a 60/30/10 frame: 60 percent of the work is traditional code and database problems, 30 percent is rule-based logic, 10 percent is genuinely a language-model problem.

Applied to a venture law practice, the numbers shift (document review and first-draft drafting are legitimately LLM-appropriate), but the discipline is the same. A typical VTG matter flow has structured-data pieces (cap table changes, filing deadlines, signature tracking) that belong on Postgres, not on DAISY. It has rule-based pieces (jurisdictional formation choices, SAFE-vs-priced decisions under a threshold) that belong on deterministic logic, not on a language model. It has judgment pieces (founder counseling, term-sheet strategy, exit timing) that belong on partners.

Naming which piece lives where is the work. The measurement layer falls out of the naming.

The relevant case study

KPMG UK, one of the Big Four

We ran this exact exercise for KPMG UK (Big Four) in 2025. 40-plus executives, regulated-industry audience, senior buyer seat. The deliverable was a framework the firm could carry into its own client conversations. We did not build tooling for them. We gave them the method.

The shape of the engagement is a small number of working sessions with your practice leadership, a measurement frame, and an artifact you can hand to a portfolio founder when they ask. Scope is a short, senior-level engagement sized for a global practice chair's calendar.

The relevant paper

Interpretable Context Methodology, submitted to ACM TiiS, MIT-licensed on GitHub (repo). Folder structure as agent architecture. Useful because it gives Dentons a methodology Dentons did not have to build, published through a venue partners actually respect.

Since this is a venture and technology practice that will also look at AI-governance artifacts, a companion piece sits at arxiv.org/abs/2510.11742 with an open repo at github.com/RinDig/AuditEngine. The Ethics Engine is a psychometric assessment tool for evaluating ideological and moral patterns in LLMs, which maps to the firm's cyber and privacy bench.
